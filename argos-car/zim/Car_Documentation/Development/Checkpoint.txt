===== Current Pin Mapping - Raspberry Pi Zero W =====

==== GPIO Pin Usage ====

|| Component | Physical Pin | GPIO | Function |
||-----------|--------------|------|----------|
|| **Motor Controller (L298N)** ||||
|| GND (Motors) | Pin 6 | GND | Ground reference motors |
|| GND (Motors) | Pin 14 | GND | Ground reference motors |
|| ENA | Pin 10 | GPIO 8 | Motor A Enable (PWM) |
|| ENB | Pin 12 | GPIO 18 | Motor B Enable (PWM) |
|| IN1 | Pin 11 | GPIO 17 | Motor A Direction 1 |
|| IN2 | Pin 13 | GPIO 27 | Motor A Direction 2 |
|| IN3 | Pin 15 | GPIO 22 | Motor B Direction 1 |
|| IN4 | Pin 16 | GPIO 23 | Motor B Direction 2 |
|| **HC-SR04 Ultrasonic Sensor** ||||
|| GND (Sensor) | Pin 9 | GND | Ground reference sensor |
|| TRIG | Pin 18 | GPIO 24 | Sensor trigger |
|| ECHO | Pin 19 | GPIO 10 | Sensor echo |

**Notes:**
* Multiple GND pins used to distribute current safely (Pin 6, 9, 14)
* ENA and ENB connected for PWM speed control
* L298N powered with 12V battery (5V_EN jumper INSTALLED - factory default)
* 12V battery connects directly to L298N "+12V" and "GND" pins
* L298N "+5V" output pin is NOT used (internal regulator provides logic power)

===== Steering System Redesign =====

==== Initial Servo Motor Approach (ABANDONED) ====

**Original Plan:**
* Use servo motor for front wheel steering
* Standard car-like steering mechanism

**Problems Encountered:**

===== Problem 1: Zero Point Discovery =====
* **Issue:** Servo motor initial angle was unknown
* **Impact:** When commanding "turn right", servo would move from zero point to random position
* **Result:** When car steering was physically straight, servo was NOT at zero angle
* **Solution Required:** Had to discover the actual initial angle of the servo before programming turns

===== Problem 2: Servo Motor Quality Issues =====
* **Issue:** Servo motor exhibited erratic, shaky movement
* **Symptom:** When turning left or right, motor vibrated and did not execute clean, smooth movements
* **Analysis:** Servo motor is fragile and not suitable for this project's requirements
* **Realization:** Programming improvements alone would not fix the hardware limitation

**Decision:** ✅ **COMPLETELY REMOVE SERVO MOTOR FROM PROJECT**

---

==== New Steering Solution: Differential Drive ====

===== The Brilliant Idea =====

Instead of using a servo motor for steering, use **inverted traction between wheels** to control direction.

**Discovery:** This technique already exists and is called **DIFFERENTIAL DRIVE (Differential Traction)**

**How It Works:**
* Left motor forward + Right motor backward = Turn right
* Right motor forward + Left motor backward = Turn left
* Both motors forward = Straight ahead
* Both motors backward = Reverse

===== Why This Works for ARGOS =====

**Advantages:**
1. ✅ **Motors already installed** - no new hardware needed
2. ✅ **12V motors are powerful** - sufficient torque for differential steering
3. ✅ **Independent power supply** - 12V battery dedicated to motors
4. ✅ **Software-controlled** - just needs proper programming logic
5. ✅ **No fragile servo** - eliminates unreliable component

**Implementation Status:** 🔄 Logic programming in progress

===== Front Wheel Challenge =====

==== Problem: Fixed Front Wheels ====

**Issue:** Front wheels are fixed (do not rotate sideways), creating more work for rear motors to execute turns.

**Analysis:**
* Fixed front wheels increase friction during turns
* Rear motors must work harder to pivot the car
* Could affect turning radius and smoothness

===== Solutions Applied =====

**Solution 1: Limited Turning Requirements**
* ✅ For project presentation, car does NOT need complex curves
* ✅ Only needs slight obstacle deviation maneuvers
* ✅ Differential drive is sufficient for required movements

**Solution 2: Wheel Friction Optimization**
* **All wheels:** 3D printed (naturally low friction with floor)
* **Rear wheels:** Add rubber/foam to increase traction
* **Front wheels:** Keep low friction (will slide during turns)
* **Result:** Front wheels slide, rear wheels grip, improving turn efficiency

**Expected Outcome:** Higher percentage of successful turning maneuvers

===== Lessons Learned =====

1. **Hardware Quality Matters:**
   * Cheap servo motor caused more problems than it solved
   * Sometimes the best solution is to remove problematic components

2. **Simpler Can Be Better:**
   * Differential drive is mechanically simpler than servo steering
   * Uses existing hardware instead of adding complexity

3. **Work With What You Have:**
   * Fixed front wheels are not ideal, but acceptable for project scope
   * Friction optimization can compensate for mechanical limitations

4. **Stage-Based Development:**
   * Validate basic functionality before adding advanced features
   * Reduces debugging complexity when problems occur
